The most common cover letter advice assumes you have a career to talk about.

"Highlight your achievements." "Quantify your impact." "Show how your experience at Company A prepared you for Company B." That's great advice — for someone who's had a Company A. When you're a new graduate, your professional experience section is either blank, a handful of internships, or a part-time job that has nothing to do with the role you want.

So you're stuck staring at a blank page, trying to write convincingly about experience you don't have yet. And that's where most new grads go wrong: they try to play a game they can't win. They stretch a three-month internship into something it wasn't, or they fill paragraphs with vague enthusiasm that could apply to literally any job at any company.

There's a better approach. But it requires you to stop thinking like an experienced professional and start thinking like what you actually are — someone with real skills, real projects, and a real perspective, just not the traditional packaging.

## Why New Grads Need a Different Strategy

Here's the reality hiring managers won't say out loud: they already know you don't have much experience. They read "Class of 2026" on your resume. They're not expecting five years of industry expertise.

What they *are* looking for is evidence that you can think, learn, and do the work. They want signals — anything that suggests you'll be competent on day one and growing by day thirty. Your cover letter needs to provide those signals using different raw material than a mid-career professional would use.

**The fundamental shift: instead of proving you've done the job before, you're proving you're ready to do it now.**

That means leading with projects instead of positions. Coursework instead of career progression. Demonstrated skills instead of job titles. It's a different argument, but it's not a weaker one — if you make it well.

## What You Actually Have (More Than You Think)

New grads tend to discount everything that didn't come with a paycheck. That's a mistake. Here's what counts:

**Academic projects.** That database you built for your capstone. The marketing plan you developed for a real local business in your strategy class. The research paper where you analyzed three years of survey data. These are real work products that demonstrate real skills.

**Internships and co-ops.** Even short ones. Even ones where you mostly shadowed. If you touched actual work — wrote code that shipped, drafted copy that got published, analyzed data that informed a decision — that's experience.

**Volunteer and extracurricular work.** Running the finance club's budget is financial experience. Managing a student organization's social media is marketing experience. Organizing a hackathon for 200 people is project management experience. The fact that you weren't paid doesn't erase the skills.

**Personal projects.** The app you built on weekends. The blog you maintained. The freelance design work you did for friends' small businesses. Self-directed projects often demonstrate more initiative than internships, because nobody told you to do them.

**Relevant coursework.** Not a list of course titles — that's boring and unhelpful. But specific things you learned and applied. "In my data structures course, I built a search algorithm that processed 10,000 records in under two seconds" is a lot more useful than "Completed CS 301: Data Structures."

## The Structure: Interest, Evidence, Fit

Experienced professionals use a "here's what I've done" structure. New grads need an "interest, evidence, fit" structure.

### Opening: Genuine Interest (2-3 sentences)

Why this company? Why this role? Not "I've always been passionate about technology" — that's meaningless. Something specific. Maybe you used their product. Maybe you read their engineering blog. Maybe their mission connects to something you studied or care about. The opening should make the reader think: "This person actually chose to apply here, specifically."

### Middle: Evidence You Can Do the Work (2-3 short paragraphs)

This is the core. Pick two or three things from your background — projects, coursework, internships, volunteer work — that directly connect to what the job requires. Don't just list them. Explain what you did, what it required, and what the result was. Be specific enough that the reader can picture you doing similar work for them.

The key word is *connect*. Every piece of evidence should link back to something in the job description. If the role asks for data analysis, talk about your research project. If it asks for teamwork, talk about the group project where you coordinated four people across different time zones. Make the connection explicit — don't make the hiring manager guess.

### Close: Why You're a Fit (2-3 sentences)

End with a forward-looking statement. Not "I look forward to hearing from you" — that's filler. Something that shows you've thought about what you'd actually contribute. What would you bring on day one? What are you eager to learn? End on a note that makes them want to have a conversation.

## Example 1: CS Graduate Applying to a Junior Developer Role

> Hi Jamie,
>
> I've been following Raycast's developer blog for the past year, especially the series on building performant React components. The post about reducing bundle size by 60% through lazy loading is actually what pushed me to refactor my own capstone project — and the results convinced me that writing clean, performance-aware code is what I want to spend my career doing.
>
> For my senior capstone, I built a full-stack inventory management app for a local restaurant that was tracking everything in spreadsheets. I wrote the backend in Node.js with PostgreSQL, built the frontend in React, and deployed it on AWS. The app handled 500+ menu items across three locations, and the owner told me it cut their weekly inventory time from four hours to forty minutes. Beyond the technical work, the project taught me how to scope requirements from a non-technical stakeholder, make tradeoffs when time was limited, and write documentation that someone without a CS degree could follow.
>
> I also contributed to two open-source projects during my final semester — fixing a pagination bug in a popular React table library and adding TypeScript types to a Node.js middleware package. Both PRs were merged, and the process taught me more about code review and collaborative development than any course could.
>
> I'd love to bring that same combination of technical execution and user focus to Raycast's frontend team. I'm especially interested in the performance optimization challenges that come with building developer tools — software for people who notice when something is slow.

**Why this works:** The opening proves genuine interest with a specific detail — not "I love your company" but "I read your blog and it changed how I code." The capstone project shows full-stack ability with a real user and a measurable result. The open-source contributions demonstrate collaboration skills. The close ties his interests directly to the team's actual work.

## Example 2: Liberal Arts Graduate Applying to a Marketing Coordinator Role

> Hi Priya,
>
> When I saw the Marketing Coordinator role at Greenhouse, what caught my attention wasn't the job title — it was the line about "telling stories that make complex products feel simple." I spent four years studying how narratives shape understanding, and I've been applying that skill to marketing for the past two years, just without the job title to match.
>
> As editor of our university's magazine, I managed a team of twelve writers and redesigned our content strategy to focus on student entrepreneurship stories. We grew our Instagram following from 800 to 4,500 in one academic year — not through ads, but through consistent storytelling that our audience actually wanted to share. I also ran the social media for a local nonprofit during a fundraising campaign, creating a content calendar, writing weekly email newsletters, and producing short-form video that helped them exceed their $50,000 goal by 15%.
>
> My English degree taught me to analyze audience, structure arguments, and edit ruthlessly — which turns out to be exactly what good marketing copy requires. I also completed HubSpot's Inbound Marketing and Content Marketing certifications to make sure I could pair the writing instincts with the strategic framework.
>
> I'm drawn to Greenhouse because you're marketing to HR professionals who are skeptical of jargon and short on time — an audience where clear, honest writing is a competitive advantage. I'd love to discuss how I could contribute to that voice.

**Why this works:** She doesn't apologize for being an English major — she positions it as an asset. The magazine and nonprofit examples demonstrate real marketing skills with real numbers. The certifications show she's done the work to bridge the gap between her degree and the industry. The close demonstrates she's researched the company's audience.

## Three Mistakes New Grads Make Constantly

### 1. Apologizing for Being New

"I know I don't have much experience, but..." is the new grad equivalent of walking into an interview and saying "you probably shouldn't hire me." The hiring manager posted an entry-level job. They know they're getting someone early in their career. You don't need to remind them — and you definitely don't need to frame it as a weakness.

### 2. Being So Generic It Could Apply Anywhere

"I'm a hard worker with strong communication skills and a passion for innovation." That sentence appears in roughly ten thousand cover letters per day, and it tells a hiring manager absolutely nothing. If you could copy-paste your cover letter to five different companies without changing a word, it's too generic. Every letter should have at least one detail that only works for *this* specific role at *this* specific company.

### 3. Overselling or Inflating

The temptation to make a class project sound like a Fortune 500 engagement is real. Resist it. Hiring managers can smell exaggeration, and it destroys trust instantly. A well-described small project is far more impressive than a poorly disguised attempt to make your coursework sound like professional experience. Be honest about the scale and let the skills speak for themselves.

## The Blank Page Problem

The hardest part of writing a cover letter as a new grad isn't the lack of experience — it's knowing where to start. You have projects, coursework, skills, and ideas, but organizing them into a compelling argument for a specific job takes time and a lot of staring at a cursor.
