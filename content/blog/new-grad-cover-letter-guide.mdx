The most common cover letter advice assumes you have a career to talk about.

"Highlight your achievements." "Quantify your impact." "Show how your experience at Company A prepared you for Company B." Great advice — for someone who's had a Company A. When you're writing a new grad cover letter, your professional experience section is either blank, a handful of internships, or a part-time job that has nothing to do with the role you want.

So you're stuck staring at a blank page, trying to write a cover letter with no experience to draw from. And that's where most new grads go wrong: they try to play a game they can't win. They stretch a three-month internship into something it wasn't, or fill paragraphs with vague enthusiasm that could apply to any job at any company.

Here's the thing, though: you *do* have experience. You just don't recognize it yet because you're defining "experience" as "employment." That's the wrong definition, and it's the reason your first job cover letter feels impossible to write. Fix that mental model and the blank page problem mostly solves itself.

## Why the Standard Entry Level Cover Letter Advice Fails

Here's the reality hiring managers won't say out loud: they already know you don't have much experience. They read "Class of 2026" on your resume. They're not expecting five years of expertise.

What they *are* looking for is evidence that you can think, learn, and do the work. They want signals — anything that suggests you'll be competent on day one and growing by day thirty. Your cover letter needs to provide those signals using different raw material.

**The fundamental shift: instead of proving you've done the job before, you're proving you're ready to do it now.**

That means leading with projects instead of positions. Coursework instead of career progression. Demonstrated skills instead of job titles. It's a different argument, but not a weaker one — if you make it well.

## What You Actually Have (More Than You Think)

New grads tend to discount everything that didn't come with a paycheck. That's a mistake. Here's what counts:

**Academic projects.** That database you built for your capstone. The marketing plan you developed for a real local business in your strategy class. The research paper where you analyzed three years of survey data. These are real work products.

**Internships and co-ops.** Even short ones. Even ones where you mostly shadowed. If you wrote code that shipped, drafted copy that got published, or analyzed data that informed decisions — that's experience.

**Volunteer and extracurricular work.** Running the finance club's budget is financial experience. Managing a student organization's social media is marketing experience. Organizing a hackathon for 200 people is project management experience. Not being paid doesn't erase the skills.

**Personal projects.** The app you built on weekends. The blog you maintained. The freelance design work you did for friends' businesses. Self-directed projects often demonstrate more initiative than internships, because nobody told you to do them.

**Relevant coursework.** Not a list of course titles. Specific things you learned and applied. "In my data structures course, I built a search algorithm that processed 10,000 records in under two seconds" beats "Completed CS 301: Data Structures" every time.

## The Structure: Interest, Evidence, Fit

Experienced professionals use a "here's what I've done" structure. For a cover letter with no experience to lean on, you need something different: interest, evidence, fit.

### Opening: Genuine Interest (2-3 sentences)

Why this company? Why this role? Not "I've always been passionate about technology" — that's meaningless. Something specific. Maybe you used their product. Maybe you read their engineering blog. Maybe their mission connects to something you care about. The reader should think: "This person actually chose to apply here, specifically."

### Middle: Evidence You Can Do the Work (2-3 short paragraphs)

This is the core. Pick two or three things from your background — projects, coursework, internships, volunteer work — that directly connect to what the job requires. Don't just list them. Explain what you did, what it required, and what the result was.

The key word is *connect*. Every piece of evidence should link back to something in the job description. If the role asks for data analysis, talk about your research project. If it asks for teamwork, talk about the group project where you coordinated four people across time zones. Make the connection explicit.

### Close: Why You're a Fit (2-3 sentences)

End with a forward-looking statement. Not "I look forward to hearing from you" — that's filler. Show you've thought about what you'd actually contribute. What would you bring on day one? What are you eager to learn?

## Example 1: CS Graduate Applying to a Junior Developer Role

> Hi Jamie,
>
> I've been following Raycast's developer blog for the past year, especially the series on building performant React components. The post about reducing bundle size by 60% through lazy loading is actually what pushed me to refactor my own capstone project — and the results convinced me that writing clean, performance-aware code is what I want to spend my career doing.
>
> For my senior capstone, I built a full-stack inventory management app for a local restaurant that was tracking everything in spreadsheets. I wrote the backend in Node.js with PostgreSQL, built the frontend in React, and deployed it on AWS. The app handled 500+ menu items across three locations, and the owner told me it cut their weekly inventory time from four hours to forty minutes. Beyond the technical work, the project taught me how to scope requirements from a non-technical stakeholder, make tradeoffs when time was limited, and write documentation that someone without a CS degree could follow.
>
> I also contributed to two open-source projects during my final semester — fixing a pagination bug in a popular React table library and adding TypeScript types to a Node.js middleware package. Both PRs were merged, and the process taught me more about code review and collaborative development than any course could.
>
> I'd love to bring that same combination of technical execution and user focus to Raycast's frontend team. I'm especially interested in the performance optimization challenges that come with building developer tools — software for people who notice when something is slow.

**Why this works:** Genuine interest proven with a specific detail. The capstone shows full-stack ability with a real user and measurable result. Open-source contributions demonstrate collaboration. The close ties directly to the team's work.

## Example 2: Liberal Arts Graduate Applying to a Marketing Coordinator Role

> Hi Priya,
>
> When I saw the Marketing Coordinator role at Greenhouse, what caught my attention wasn't the job title — it was the line about "telling stories that make complex products feel simple." I spent four years studying how narratives shape understanding, and I've been applying that skill to marketing for the past two years, just without the job title to match.
>
> As editor of our university's magazine, I managed a team of twelve writers and redesigned our content strategy to focus on student entrepreneurship stories. We grew our Instagram following from 800 to 4,500 in one academic year — not through ads, but through consistent storytelling that our audience actually wanted to share. I also ran the social media for a local nonprofit during a fundraising campaign, creating a content calendar, writing weekly email newsletters, and producing short-form video that helped them exceed their $50,000 goal by 15%.
>
> My English degree taught me to analyze audience, structure arguments, and edit ruthlessly — which turns out to be exactly what good marketing copy requires. I also completed HubSpot's Inbound Marketing and Content Marketing certifications to make sure I could pair the writing instincts with the strategic framework.
>
> I'm drawn to Greenhouse because you're marketing to HR professionals who are skeptical of jargon and short on time — an audience where clear, honest writing is a competitive advantage. I'd love to discuss how I could contribute to that voice.

**Why this works:** She doesn't apologize for being an English major — she positions it as an asset. The magazine and nonprofit examples show real marketing skills with real numbers. Certifications bridge degree and industry. The close shows she's researched the audience.

## Three Mistakes New Grads Make Constantly

### 1. Apologizing for Being New

"I know I don't have much experience, but..." is the new grad equivalent of walking into an interview saying "you probably shouldn't hire me." The hiring manager posted an entry-level job. They know they're getting someone early in their career. You don't need to remind them, and you definitely don't need to frame it as a weakness.

### 2. Being So Generic It Could Apply Anywhere

"I'm a hard worker with strong communication skills and a passion for innovation." That sentence appears in roughly ten thousand cover letters per day, and it tells a hiring manager nothing. If you could send your cover letter to five different companies without changing a word, it's too generic. Every letter needs at least one detail that only works for *this* specific role at *this* specific company.

### 3. Copy-Pasting a Template You Found Online

You Googled "new grad cover letter template," found one that looked professional, swapped in your name and the company, and called it done. The hiring manager has seen that template before — possibly three times today. Templates strip out everything that makes you interesting and replace it with corporate filler. Use a structure, yes. Use someone else's words, no.
